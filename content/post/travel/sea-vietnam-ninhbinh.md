It seems like I had been planning a solo backpacking trip overseas for ages. In late May 2016, I finally took off for a trip through South East Asia like I had always wanted to. I was dismissive of all deterring arguments. I cared not what was happening to me or around me. This trip was all that mattered right now.

I left Hanoi for Ninh Binh, a small town two hours south of the capital, best known for it's remarkable limestone [karst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karst) landscape.

<hr />

## Ninh Binh

The bus dropped me 2 kilometers before the bus stop. They probably thought I wanted to go to Tam Coc, where most of the backpackers head. I was flustered but I had not yet mastered the art of conveying what I wanted to say in the lack of a common language. I would learn that later but for now, I was pushed out by the conductor with a reassuring smile that this was where I wanted to get off while I kept pointing straight ahead. Google Maps was not quick enough to load and I found myself looking the the back of the bus as I figured out I would have to walk for the next twenty minutes to get to the hostel.

It wasn't so bad despite the sultry heat. The hostel was a really nice one. I knew that it was the renovated old station building and that explained why it was so generously spacious and airy. The dorm room was alright with all the required facilities. Good choice! I saw a rare member of the pedigree Indian traveller while I was checking in. As is customary, we refused to acknowledge each other's existence and he was checking out of the hostel anyway so there was no need to fear a further interaction. Amusingly, we were introduced by a common friend on Facebook that very night! Sometimes you have to hand it over to the universe; it has an amazing sense of humour.

It was too late in the day to do any sightseeing so I decided to wander around. It seemed to me that the city had grown without sufficient reason. There were empty and wide roads all around. It looked like a hollow city. I walked up to the new railway station. It was in the middle of barren lands and seemed grander than it should have been. I walked in to find out how I could book a ticket to my next destination. It seemed a simple enough procedure, although I could not book a ticket then and there because I did not have my passport on me. There was a tout who was offering to sell me a ticket but I did not quite understand why anyone would use a tout for something so simple.

I wandered through the lanes of the empty city. The kids here were amazingly friendly. You encounter a hello almost every time you cross a kid on the streets. There are usually more hellos and a few questions that follow if you choose to reply and engage in a conversation of limited vocabulary. Some kids run away after hurling the hello at you so you wonder at times where it came from.

Not able to find interesting people in the hostel, I went walking once again in the evening. I first ate some delicious *banh xeo* with *nem lui*. You are given a tray full of ingredients and are supposed to roll them up into a tasty snack. I came back for it once again on my last day in town.

It was a big park full of locals who were starting to pour in for their evening walks and jogs. The ill maintained park had a mini-zoo and swings as well. Seemed like the recreational hub of the town. There were some elderly women standing in a line and giving a back massage to the one in front. ## Karst Landscapes

### Trang An

The next day I rented a bicycle from the hostel. The bicycles was rather expensive, even for a mountain bike. It cost me 70 kVND while a motorbike was 100 kVND. However, I wanted to do something physical as I had been feeling sloppy of late.

There are two places around Ninh Binh that boast of the limestone karst landscape : **Tam Coc** and **Trang An**. Of these, Tang An is the less touristy one and a UNESCO world heritage site as well. So I rode towards Trang An in the relentless sun. It burned me despite the dark tan I had already acquired. The limestone rocks presented themselves a little out of Ninh Binh. They looked amazing, a rock standing abruptly in the middle of a plain. I actually had no idea what to expect from Trang An as I had left that bit in my research as a (hopefully pleasant) surprise. I also kept looking for a place that served local specialty *De Nui* (mountain goat) but ended up not having it anywhere.

> Pro Tip #?

> Always leave a certain gap in your research in order to enjoy the place that you are visiting. For historical places, you might want to read up a bit but don't see a hundred pictures of the landscapes you are about to visit!

At Trang An, I parked the bike by paying 15 kVND. This felt rather stupid because bicycles should be free or negligible to park. I don't know why but it just seems wrong to charge bicycles and motorbikes the same amount! I also bought the traditional conical hat in my typical style of taking precautions *after* I had been burnt. The entry ticket cost me 150 kVND. It seemed somewhat steep at first but was more than justified later. It included the price of the boat that was to take me around. A boat can easily take 4 people so I had to wait a little before being adjusted with a Vietnamese family that spread out across three boats. They seemed rather reluctant to have me initially but gave me a seat to sit in.

<p class="postimg">

We set out on the rowboats with the boats-woman rowing from behind and me and another guy helping out with single paddles from the front. The sun was harsh, the water was cool and the scenery was amazing. Out three boats stuck together as we rowed around the lake amongst the huge limestone rocks. Signs of civilization have been found in these rocks and there is an occasional structure looming here and there. We came upon a temple and the Vietnamese family talked to the boats-woman in their language. I had no idea what they exchanged but we ended skipping the temple. I was not complaining. I had had seen enough of temples anyway.

<p class="postimg">

I was expecting this to be the end of the trip and us to go back but we went forward. I could not see a way forward, just a big rock that we seemed to be heading straight to. As we got closer, I could see a small gap under the rock, but were we heading there for a swim?

As we got closer, the first boat did not slow down. It slipped right into the seemingly impossibly small gap! Our boat went in as well. These river tunnels were extremely exciting and amazing. The ceiling was inches away from the head and at times you needed to duck to evade the occasional downward bulge.

There was electric lighting to aid visibility as well. The boats-women oared like pros. Almost all the boats were handled by women and this was quite a physically taxing task!

It got somewhat tedious after a while but that was probably because of the weather. The hot and humid climate made the stretches between the tunnels rather painful. The lack of a wind did not help. We took breaks in the shade of the caves and skipped all the temples. The entire trip lasted two and a half hours and I just wonder how much longer would it have been had we stopped at every temple on the way. The Vietnamese family had warmed up to my presence and offered me water and some snacks. I had come ill prepared because I was expecting the trip to be much shorter! There are shops set up in the temple complexes though so one could stop there to pick up some food and drinks.

Back at the wharf I tipped the boats-woman an amount similar to what the family had given and sat to cool down. Once I had had enough rest, I got my bike after some trouble. The parking attendant had changed and the new one was asking for money that I had already paid to the previous attendant. I tried explaining but she did not understand English. After much frustration, it was here that I realised the power of the Google Translate app. I pulled it out, typed in what I wanted to say, translated it and handed it to her. She smiled and let me out. It was really that simple!

> **Pro Tip #?**

> Don't be frustrated at the lack of a common language. Technology makes things simple. Many people can read their language despite not being able to understand your's!

I decided to go to Tam Coc next to see the area. I rode through the internal roads which were not as hot as the barren highways. The countryside was unspectacular for be though. It was a typical village and I have seen many of the kind.

### Bich Dong Pagoda

Tam Coc turned out to be very much a backpacker ghetto. I had initially thought of eating there but the burger joints and pizzerias made me change my mind. I pedalled on and reached the **Bich Dong Pagoda** at the end of the Tam Coc road. There was a 10 kVND parking fee here as well! All this was ruffling me up. But the pagoda was nice enough to calm me down.

I try to sell India at every opportunity I get!


## Vietnamese Railway

I woke up early for the Copa match. The unimpressive breakfast at the hostel was rather expensive. Nils came around and we started talking again. I had told him about my plans to do the caves at Dong Hoi and he was interested as well. I told him that I was planning to book my train tickets and told him how he could book his using [baolau](http://www.baolau.vn/). But he got busy with a girl who was hitting on him so I let them be. Young love! ðŸ˜„

I checked out of the hostel and dumped my bag in their storage room. I told Nils that I would be around since my train left late in the night. Nils was planning on going to Tam Coc so we would not have a chance to sync up till much later in the day. I went back to the railway station to book my ticket. The hat really helped! Despite the sun, I was at relative ease when I reached the station. There was no one at the counter though. I sat for a good 15-20 minutes before I decided to go out and seek some *Bun Cha* which I had started craving somewhat. But there was no *Bun Cha* to be found. In fact, there was no decent food option around which is rather weird for a railway station. When I returned to the station, I found a lady at the counter and booked my ticket for Dong Hoi despite the rickety communication. We used a pen and a paper to explain ourselves to one another. I got a hard seat in the sleeper class, an upper berth which I actually prefer, for 369 kVND.

The market had vegetables, meat, fruits, flowers, clothes, shoes and more, all in whole sale! I walked around and returned with the satisfaction of having had found the people.

I ran into an alley which had been converted into a badminton court. There were men in their 60's or 70's who were playing and I found their athleticism quite remarkable for their age. They were looking at me with suspicion though so I decided to stop hanging around in the shadows and leave them to their game.

Ricardo came around as I sat writing in the common room. We talked about his day and then he asked me if I played Chinese Chess. I did not know that there was a Chinese version of the game! Unfortunately we could not find it in the hostel so we had to leave it be. We sat to watch the ongoing football match and talked some more. He would have been one cool person to stay in touch with but he did not have any online accounts. Nils' friend came up to me to give me his contact. He had gone out but had asked his friends get in touch with me. I left him a message and then left for the station around 10:15 PM. My train was at 11 in the night. I was hoping to find Nils there.

The roads were awfully quiet. I realised that there were no noisy drunk men or barking street dogs which gave the streets a haunted silence. The sky was clouded but there was no wind, as usual. I got to the station and sat waiting with a number of other Vietnamese people. No Nils though. The gates opened 5 minutes before the train was due. The people flocked on to the platform and waited for the train to arrive. It did so shortly afterwards and I walked up to my coach where an official checked my ticket and I was admitted inside.

The cabins were like the first class category of India and I found mine. The upper berth of the three tiered sleepers in Vietnam almost touches the ceiling! There was very little room for maneuvering. The freezing air-conditioner had a vent right above my head and I had trouble falling asleep. I did sleep eventually but it was intermittently broken with the fear of missing my station. I kept checking my phone every hour to see if it was time for arrival at Dong Hoi. I finally got out of bed at 7 and sat down with the rest of the members of the compartment. Vendors came around to sell breakfast and water.

The entire arrangement seemed pretty nice. However, I did not quite understand why something so basic as the railways was made so luxurious and expensive. If they could remove the air-conditioning to slash down the rates would that not be more beneficial to the society in general? Everything in Vietnam was air-conditioned; buses, trains, taxis. I did not get the need for it.
